Ferdinand II of Aragon was a 15th-century Spanish monarch who played a central role in the formation of modern Spain. Born in 1452, he became King of Aragon in 1479 and is best known for his marriage to Isabella I of Castile in 1469, a union that effectively joined the two most powerful kingdoms on the Iberian Peninsula under one royal partnership. Although Castile and Aragon remained legally distinct, their joint rule laid the foundation for a unified Spain. Together, Ferdinand and Isabella completed the Reconquista in 1492 by conquering Granada, the last Muslim-ruled territory in Spain, ending centuries of Islamic political presence in Iberia. That same year, they financed Christopher Columbus’s voyage, which led to Spain’s vast overseas empire in the Americas. Ferdinand also strengthened royal authority, expanded Spanish influence in Italy, and supported the Spanish Inquisition, during which Jews were expelled and religious conformity was enforced. Through strategic political decisions and calculated diplomacy, Ferdinand helped transform Spain into a major European and global power.
